\begin{abstract}
Multi-camera person tracking is essential for security and operational monitoring in large-scale environments such as university campuses and industrial facilities. Existing approaches rely entirely on appearance-based re-identification (Re-ID) for cross-camera association, which is computationally expensive and degrades when subjects wear similar clothing, as is common in factory settings. We propose SpotOn, a real-time multi-camera person tracking system that addresses these limitations through a two-tier identity matching strategy. For cameras with overlapping fields of view, identities are resolved geometrically using a homography-based spatial matching module that projects detected foot positions onto a shared 2D floor plan; matching is performed via Euclidean distance minimization using the Hungarian algorithm. For non-overlapping blind spots, an appearance-based Re-ID module using a YOLOv26m detector, ByteTrack tracker, and OSNet-AIN backbone is triggered only when a tracked person enters a 5\% camera-edge zone, extracting 512-dimensional embeddings for cosine similarity matching. The system was evaluated on the MTMMC dataset across a university campus and an industrial factory, achieving an average Multiple Object Tracking Accuracy (MOTA) of 54.3\%, IDF1 of 61.8\%, and a per-frame processing latency of 49.4~ms across four concurrent camera streams. Future work will focus on automating homography calibration and validating the system on additional real-world multi-camera benchmarks.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Multi-camera person tracking is a cornerstone technology for modern smart cities, intelligent transportation hubs, retail analytics, and vast public spaces. In these diverse real-world applications, maintaining a continuous trajectory of an individual as they move across disjointed camera networks is essential for ensuring public safety, optimizing crowd flow, and extracting operational intelligence. However, as the scale of surveillance networks expands, traditional manual monitoring becomes computationally intractable and highly prone to human error, necessitating the development of robust, automated tracking frameworks.

The fundamental challenge in multi-camera tracking lies in the visual instability of subjects across varying physical environments. When an individual transitions from one camera view to another, lighting fluctuations, perspective distortions, and severe partial occlusions can drastically alter their physical appearance. Existing tracking systems typically resolve this by brute-forcing cross-camera association using computationally expensive appearance-based re-identification (Re-ID) on every frame. This brute-force approach severely limits real-time scalability. Furthermore, appearance-based models degrade significantly in environments where subjects wear highly similar clothing---such as uniform security personnel or industrial factory workers---rendering purely visual matching unreliable.

In this paper, we propose SpotOn, a real-time multi-camera person tracking system that uses a two-tier matching strategy. For cameras with overlapping fields of view, identities are resolved geometrically using homography-based spatial matching, which is faster and more robust than appearance-based Re-ID in such zones. For non-overlapping blind spots, the Re-ID module is activated only when a person approaches the edge margin of a camera frame. This design reduces overall computational cost while maintaining tracking accuracy.

The SpotOn pipeline consists of four tightly integrated stages. First, a YOLOv26m object detector processes each camera frame to localize all persons within the scene. Second, ByteTrack maintains per-camera track identities across frames using Kalman filtering and IoU-based association, preserving continuity through short occlusions. Third, for cameras with overlapping fields of view, a homography-based spatial matching module projects the foot position of each detection onto a shared 2D floor plan; identities are then resolved by computing pairwise Euclidean distances and applying the Hungarian algorithm across camera views. Fourth, for non-overlapping camera pairs, an appearance-based Re-ID module using OSNet-AIN is triggered only when a track enters a 5\% edge-margin zone, extracting a 512-dimensional embedding for cross-camera matching via cosine similarity. Each stage is described in detail in Section~\ref{sec:methodology}.

The main contributions of this work are: (1) a two-tier identity matching strategy that prioritizes geometry-based matching for overlapping views and invokes Re-ID only at camera edge boundaries; (2) a homography-based spatial matching module that projects detections onto a shared 2D floor plan and resolves identities without appearance features; and (3) an evaluation on the MTMMC dataset~\cite{mtmmc} demonstrating competitive IDF1, MOTA, and sub-second per-frame latency.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section II reviews related work on multi-camera tracking and geometric identity resolution. Section III describes the proposed system methodology. Section IV presents the experimental setup and results. Section V concludes the paper.

\section{Related Works}
\label{sec:related-works}

Multi-camera person tracking requires solving several interconnected sub-problems: detecting persons in each frame, tracking them within a single camera, and re-establishing their identities across cameras.

\textit{Multi-Camera Person Tracking.}
Wojke et al.~\cite{deepsort} proposed DeepSORT, which extends SORT~\cite{sort} by adding a deep appearance descriptor and Mahalanobis distance metric for data association, establishing the standard tracking-by-detection paradigm. Zhang et al.~\cite{strongsort} presented StrongSORT, which improves upon DeepSORT using a stronger Re-ID backbone and an exponential-moving-average embedding update strategy, achieving consistent gains on MOT17~\cite{mot17} and MOT20 benchmarks. Gautam et al.~\cite{yoloreidnet} proposed YOLORe-IDNet, which combines detection and Re-ID into a single forward pass; however, its tightly coupled design limits modularity and does not support geometry-based identity recovery in overlapping camera zones.

\textit{Geometric and Spatial Constraints.}
Wang et al.~\cite{mixed_syn_tracking} addressed track fragmentation in crowded scenes by applying Gaussian Mixture Models to split tracklets containing multiple identities, complementing spatial-aware pipelines that prevent mixing earlier. Wu et al.~\cite{geom_consist_tracking} incorporated 3D geometric constraints into multi-camera tracking as a fallback when appearance features are unreliable; our spatial matching module pursues a similar motivation but relies on 2D homography matrices that are simpler to calibrate. Hachiuma et al.~\cite{hospital_tracking} demonstrated that spatial trajectories are more reliable than visual features in appearance-constrained environments such as operating rooms, motivating our design choice to prioritize geometric matching for overlapping fields of view.

Unlike these approaches, SpotOn explicitly separates overlapping and non-overlapping camera zones with a two-tier strategy: homography-based spatial matching handles overlapping views without any appearance features, while Re-ID is reserved for non-overlapping blind spots, reducing computational cost while maintaining tracking accuracy.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

This section describes the SpotOn pipeline and each of its constituent modules. The system integrates a CNN-based detector, a tracking-by-detection algorithm, and a homography-based spatial matching layer, with an appearance-based Re-ID module reserved for non-overlapping zones.

\subsection{System Overview}
\label{subsec:system-overview}

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline}, the pipeline consists of four stages: (1) Person Detection, (2) Intra-Camera Tracking, (3) Spatial Matching via Homography, and (4) Cross-Camera Re-Identification. To facilitate seamless deployment, we developed a centralized application built with FastAPI that runs as an asynchronous service, connecting all independent processes to measure systemic performance in real time. Each camera feed is processed by an independent worker, and a central coordinator aggregates the results and maintains the global identity registry, as detailed in the deployment workflow (Fig.~\ref{fig:workflow}). Models are shared across workers to minimize GPU memory usage.

Tracking results are delivered to operator clients through two synchronized channels. Raw video frames are streamed via MJPEG, while structured tracking data---bounding boxes, identity labels, and floor plan positions---is pushed through WebSocket connections. The frontend overlays WebSocket data onto the MJPEG stream in real time.

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{subsec:datasets}

We evaluated our system on the MTMMC (Multi-Target Multi-Modal Camera) dataset \cite{mtmmc}, which provides synchronized video feeds from two environments: a university campus with outdoor pathways and an indoor factory with workstations. The campus environment utilizes four tracking cameras that exhibit viewpoint variation, natural lighting changes, and occlusions. The factory environment also utilizes four cameras, presenting distinct challenges such as uniform worker attire and repetitive background patterns.

\subsection{Person Detection}
\label{subsec:person-detection}

We use YOLOv26m~\cite{yolov26m} as the person detector. YOLOv26m employs a CNN-based architecture with a Non-Maximum Suppression (NMS)-free decoupled head that eliminates the post-processing bottleneck common in earlier YOLO variants~\cite{yolov8}, enabling efficient multi-stream inference. Input frames are resized to $480 \times 480$ pixels and normalized. We apply a confidence threshold of 0.3 to reduce missed detections that would otherwise be filtered by standard NMS-based thresholds.

\subsection{Intra-Camera Tracking}
\label{subsec:intra-camera-tracking}

Bounding boxes from the detector are passed to ByteTrack~\cite{bytetrack} for multi-object tracking within each camera. ByteTrack associates both high- and low-confidence detections with existing tracks using a two-round matching scheme, which reduces identity loss from partial occlusions compared to trackers that discard low-score detections~\cite{sort}. Each person is assigned a local Track ID maintained across frames using Kalman filtering and Intersection over Union (IoU)-based matching, with a 20-frame buffer for temporarily occluded tracks.

\subsection{Geometry-First Spatial Matching}
\label{subsec:spatial-matching}

The spatial matching module resolves identities geometrically for cameras with overlapping fields of view. We compute a $3 \times 3$ homography matrix $\mathbf{H}$ for each camera using RANdom SAmple Consensus (RANSAC) over manually defined ground-plane calibration points, as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:ground_labeling}. During tracking, the bottom-center point of each bounding box, approximating the person's foot position $(x_c, y_{max})$, is projected onto the shared floor plan:

\begin{equation}
\lambda \mathbf{p}_{map} = \mathbf{H} \mathbf{p}_{img}
\end{equation}

where $\lambda$ is a scaling factor. As depicted in Fig.~\ref{fig:spatial_matching}, the system then constructs a pairwise Euclidean distance matrix over all projected positions across camera views and applies the Hungarian algorithm to find optimal identity assignments. Pairs within 100 pixels (campus) or 200 pixels (factory) are merged under a single Global ID.

\subsection{Cross-Camera Re-Identification}
\label{subsec:re-id}

For non-overlapping camera pairs, spatial proximity cannot resolve identities. We define handoff zones as a 5\% margin along each edge of every camera frame. When a tracked person enters these zones, the system triggers the Re-ID module to extract a 512-dimensional appearance embedding using the Torchreid framework~\cite{torchreid} with an OSNet-AIN backbone~\cite{osnet}, which learns omni-scale features at multiple receptive field sizes simultaneously. The OSNet-AIN backbone yields an average 68.7\% Rank-1 and 62.0\% mAP on the MTMMC dataset~\cite{mtmmc}. Embeddings are $L_2$-normalized and stored in a Facebook AI Similarity Search (FAISS) index~\cite{faiss} with a 60-second expiry. Incoming edge detections are matched against this registry using cosine similarity; tracks exceeding a threshold of 0.70 inherit the matched Global ID.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{subsec:exp-setup}

All experiments were conducted on an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4060 GPU (8 GB VRAM). The YOLOv26m detector was accelerated using NVIDIA TensorRT with an input resolution of $480 \times 480$ pixels and a confidence threshold of 0.3. ByteTrack was configured with a 20-frame track buffer. For cross-camera matching, the Re-ID similarity threshold was set to 0.70, and the FAISS index utilized Inner Product search for 512-dimensional embeddings. To maintain real-time performance across all concurrent streams, the system was configured to process every third frame, yielding an effective processing rate of 7.67 FPS per camera.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{subsec:metrics}

To quantify the performance of the proposed pipeline, we employ standard metrics across three domains. For object detection, we report Mean Average Precision at IoU 50\% (mAP@50), Recall, and Precision. Multi-object tracking is evaluated using Multiple Object Tracking Accuracy (MOTA), which aggregates false positives, false negatives, and identity switches; the ID F1 Score (IDF1), which emphasizes identity consistency through track longevity; and Multiple Object Tracking Precision (MOTP) for bounding box localization error. Cross-camera Re-ID is assessed using Rank-1 cumulative matching accuracy and Re-ID mAP. Finally, system efficiency is evaluated through algorithmic end-to-end processing latency (ms).

\section{Experimental Results}
\label{sec:results}

This section presents the results of our evaluation. We tested the system on the MTMMC dataset \cite{mtmmc}. 

To rigorously validate our system's efficacy across diverse scenarios, we report all evaluation metrics disaggregated by individual camera feeds (e.g., c09, c12, ..., c05) rather than relying exclusively on global averages. This granular, per-camera reporting exposes how the pipeline handles explicit environmental challenges unique to distinct viewpoints---for instance, overcoming the uniform visual appearance of subjects in factory sectors (c09, c16) versus coping with extreme outdoor illumination variances on campus pathways (c01, c05). Presenting the data per-camera thus proves the robustness of both the geometric spatial matching and specific Re-ID trigger logic across highly heterogeneous fields of view.

\subsection{Detection Performance}
\label{subsec:detection-results}

Table~\ref{tab:detection} compares detection accuracy between the baseline (plain PyTorch) and the SpotOn optimized pipeline (TensorRT, frame-skip=2). The optimized YOLOv26m detector achieved a +23.2\% average mAP@50 gain in the factory environment and a +25.3\% gain in the campus environment. These improvements highlight the robustness of the updated decoupled head architecture against challenging factory backgrounds and extreme outdoor lighting variations. Furthermore, the substantial increase in recall (up to +36.7\% on campus camera c01) directly benefits downstream spatial association by preventing broken tracklets caused by missed bounding box detections.

\subsection{Tracking Performance}
\label{subsec:tracking-results}

Table~\ref{tab:tracking} presents the multi-object tracking evaluation across both environments. The optimized system significantly reduces identity switches (ID Sw.), dropping from an average of 506 to just 50 in the factory, and from 552 to 66 on campus. This drastic reduction demonstrates the effectiveness of integrating robust Kalman filtering with the homography-based spatial matching module to prevent spatial track drifts. Concurrently, average MOTA improves by +24.0\% and +26.1\% in factory and campus settings, respectively. The robust IDF1 scores exceeding 60\% across both environments confirm that tracking consistency and long-term identity maintenance remain highly accurate, even while utilizing a frame-skip strategy to boost speed.

\subsection{Re-Identification Performance}
\label{subsec:reid-results}

Table~\ref{tab:reid} details cross-camera Re-ID performance. The optimized SpotOn pipeline improves matching accuracy by confining the OSNet-AIN backbone evaluation strictly to predefined 5\% camera edge zones. By replacing exhaustive frame-by-frame feature extraction with geometry-first matching and edge-trigger Re-ID, the system actively filters out false positives caused by appearance ambiguity---such as uniform worker attire in factories. Consequently, the factory average Rank-1 accuracy improved by +3.4\%, reaching 72.8\%, while the campus environment saw a steeper +7.1\% gain due to higher variance in pedestrian clothing facilitating cleaner feature embeddings when extracted selectively.


\subsection{Processing Latency}
\label{subsec:latency-results}

Table~\ref{tab:latency} compares per-frame processing latency stage-by-stage. Accelerated by TensorRT, the SpotOn configuration drastically reduces computational overhead across every component. The most profound improvement occurs in feature extraction, where the edge-triggered Re-ID strategy circumvents unnecessary embedding computations, reducing time from 181.5 ms to 36.3 ms. Overall, total end-to-end latency drops from 233.0 ms per frame to 49.4 ms. This 4.7$\times$ speedup guarantees an estimated system capacity of 20.3 FPS, cleanly surpassing the 15 FPS threshold required for real-time situational awareness.

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{subsec:discussion}

To assess module contributions, we evaluated four configurations: (a) detection and tracking only, (b) with Re-ID added, (c) with spatial matching added, and (d) the full pipeline. As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:comparison}, spatial matching provides a larger IDF1 improvement than Re-ID alone in the campus environment, where camera views overlap. Re-ID contributes more in the factory, where views are disjoint. The full pipeline achieves the best results in both environments.

The system has two main limitations. First, tracking accuracy is sensitive to the quality of manual homography calibration; errors in calibration point selection propagate directly into floor plan projections. Second, detection reliability degrades in highly crowded scenes with persistent occlusions, leading to increased ID switches in environments such as the campus.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this paper, we presented SpotOn, a real-time multi-camera person tracking system designed for large-scale surveillance environments such as university campuses and industrial factories. The system addresses the fundamental challenge of maintaining identity continuity across distributed camera networks through a two-tier matching strategy. For cameras with overlapping fields of view, identities are resolved geometrically using a homography-based spatial matching module that projects each detection onto a shared 2D floor plan. For non-overlapping blind spots, an appearance-based Re-ID module is invoked only when a tracked person approaches the edge zone of a camera frame, substantially reducing unnecessary computation. The system was evaluated on the MTMMC dataset~\cite{mtmmc} across two distinct environments, achieving an average Multiple Object Tracking Accuracy (MOTA) of 54.3\%, IDF1 of 61.8\%, and a per-frame processing latency of 49.4 ms across four concurrent camera streams. The factory environment yielded higher MOTA (58.4\%) and IDF1 (63.3\%) than the campus (50.1\% and 60.2\%, respectively), reflecting the greater geometric regularity of indoor scenes. Ablation analysis confirmed that spatial matching provides a larger identity continuity benefit than Re-ID alone in overlapping-view environments, while Re-ID contributes more in disjoint-view configurations. Compared to a non-optimized baseline, the full pipeline reduced end-to-end latency from 233.0 ms to 49.4 ms and improved factory MOTA from 34.4\% to 58.4\%. For future work, we plan to automate the homography calibration process and explore temporal motion prediction to handle long occlusions. Validation on additional multi-camera benchmarks and real-world deployments is also planned.
